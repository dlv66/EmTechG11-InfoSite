Past

2000
In 2000’s E3 (Electronic Entertainment Expo), G-cluster showed a demonstration of their own cloud gaming technology. The original offer was a cloud gaming service over the internet to handheld devices.

2005
Video game developer Crytek began researching on their own cloud gaming system in 2005 for Crysis, but stopped development in 2007 to wait until the infrastructure and internet providers were up for the task.

2010
By March 2010, OnLive was officially launched with its game service beginning in June of the same year. Five years later, Sony Computer Entertainment acquired its patents and the provider shut down.

2011
Gaikai was internationally launched on February 27, 2011, with Dead Space 2, Mass Effect 2, Spore, and The Sims 3. On July 2 of the following year, it was acquired by Sony Computer Entertainment for $380 M USD. By 2014, Sony announced PlayStation Now.

2015
GeForce Now was launched on October 1, 2015 by Nvidia. It allows users to play games directly from the web.

2017
In 2017, the Blade SAS Group launched Shadow in France. The following year it announced that the service was live in 19 states in the West and East Coasts of the U.S.

2018
On May 22, 2018, Electronic Arts acquired GameFly, and on October 29 of the same year, the company announced Project Atlas, their new cloud gaming program.

Google launched Project Stream on October 1, 2018. From October 5, 2018 to January 15, 2019, a limited number of participants from the U.S. could play Assassin’s Creed Odyssey through Chrome. It was formally announced at the 2019 Game Developers Conference on March 19, 2019 by the name Stadia.

Project xCloud was announced by Microsoft on October 8, 2018. It aimed to incorporate Microsoft Azure cloud services into cloud gaming. As of May 2019, Kareem Choudry, the Corporate Vice President of Microsoft, announced that the project could stream 3,500 Xbox titles.

Loudplay, along with Rostelecom and Huawei, demonstrated the first 5G cloud gaming showcase in Innopolis, Russia.


Present

The latest news on cloud gaming is its introduction to Android phones. Microsoft's xCloud was shown to be able to stream halo to an Android phone. Google will also bring its Stadia service to Pixel phones. Both will be accessible this fall, which is presumambly why Nvidia recently announced that GeForce Now will be extending to Android phones. However, unlike Microsoft and Google, Nvidia has not given a release date. All these services will require you to use a bluetooth gamepad to be able to play all of the games available.
